Object Oriented Paradigm 3 - Identifying Objects and their Attributes

Identifying objects

The first step in OOP design is to identify objects. In the real world, objects are part of our everyday lives. Some of these objects are 'things' that are fairly straightforward to define, e.g. a light switch, and some are more complex to understand, both in terms of their definition, and how to approach modelling their behaviour in code as part of a computerised system, e.g. a car, bank account, or fridge.

Some level of skill is required to identify objects, but it does become easier with experience. It can help to pick out the relevant nouns in a text description of the problem. To help identify the objects in the card game, consider the first two paragraphs of the game description. Potential objects have been identified and highlighted in the passages below.

The game is played by two players with a deck of 52 cards. Cards are worth their face value, except for the ace which is worth 1 or 11 points (the player can choose). Court cards (jack, queen, and king) are worth 10 points each. A hand's value is the sum of the values of the cards. The player will play against the computer (an AI system that will be referred to as Alana). The aim of the game is to have the hand with the highest value, up to a value of 21.

At this early stage, you are just picking out the most important things in the system to model. If in doubt, you should assume that anything that exists in the real world (relating to the problem domain), that has some specific meaning, will be an object. In the next section, you will consider the player, deck, and playing card objects in more detail.

Step 2 - Identify the attributes of each object

The next step of the design process is to identify the attributes of each object. Each attribute is a data item that helps define or describe the object (like variables in procedural terms).

In the card game, a playing card has been identified as an object.

How would you describe it? A playing card has three important attributes: it has a suit and a rank that identify it (e.g. the queen of hearts), and it has a value that will be used to help determine the overall value of a player's hand (e.g. the queen of hearts has a value of 10).

A real playing card will have other properties that may be irrelevant to a computer model. For example, it might be made of plastic and have a patterned back. Initially, the list of attributes will be restricted to those that you need for this specific game: all playing cards will have a suit, rank, and value.

You can also identify the data types of each attribute.

A Player will have a lot of attributes, such as their name, hair colour, and shoe size. However, once more, you should only include the attributes that you need for the game. You can easily extend the list of attributes at a later stage, if needed.

Finally, consider a more complex object, the deck of cards. What are the attributes of a deck of cards? How would you describe it? It is just a collection of playing cards, so you can use an array to hold the collection.
